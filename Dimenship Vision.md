## Overview

Dimesnship is a single-player, offline-first, asynchronous systems strategy game set in a hard science-fiction universe where reality is layered into adjacent dimensional strata. The player is a fugitive from their Native Strata, accused of a crime supported by fabricated circumstances and incomplete records. Direct action is impossible. Vindication—if pursued at all—depends on careful planning, autonomous operations, and analytical reconstruction.

The game is built around one fundamental principle:

> The player does not control events in real time.  
> The player designs systems that operate autonomously.

All operations—missions, construction, logistics, tuning, repairs, and conflict resolution—unfold asynchronously. The player's only domain of control is preparation: building infrastructure, assembling robots, defining behavior rules, prioritizing production, and analyzing structured telemetry. Outcomes are deterministic. Failure is non-terminal. There are no reload loops, no competitive pressure, and no penalties for stepping away.

Dimesnship is an engineering game framed as a multidimensional investigation: you do not “win” through reaction speed, but through architecture.

## Setting and Narrative Foundation

Reality in Dimesnship is not singular. It consists of adjacent strata within a multidimensional phase-space—where time is one navigable dimension. Strata can be close to the Native Strata in structure and identity, yet displaced along time; the player can target time-offset strata that resemble the Native world at specific temporal positions.

Travel is constrained by physical alignment. Matter has a native alignment. The closer foreign matter approaches the Native Strata, the stronger the reversion drift pulling it back toward its origin. The player's vessel survives by maintaining a continuous stabilization field that counteracts this drift—but operating near the Native Strata becomes energetically prohibitive. This is why the protagonist cannot pursue the truth personally, and why they rely on proxies.

The player has chosen refuge in a remote, stable stratum where stabilization is affordable. From there, they dispatch autonomous robotic units into other strata to extract resources, recover rare components, and gather information.

The narrative is not built from cinematic sequences, nor from collecting admissible “evidence.” Information from altered strata cannot be trusted as proof. Instead, the player returns with leads: potentially correct discoveries that can be verified indirectly in the Native Strata—witness names, locations, timelines, and hints about where physical proof may exist. The player can send these leads to outside investigators through minimal contact channels, prompting native-world verification without revealing their position.

Truth is not retrieved. It is inferred.

## Core Experience

Dimesnship revolves around three intertwined activities:

- Designing autonomous systems (robots and behaviors)
- Managing a self-sustaining industrial base (energy, logistics, facilities)
- Analyzing logs and reports to refine strategy and progress objectives

The player's vessel is both sanctuary and production platform. It generates energy, fabricates components, maintains robots, and schedules industrial work. Progress continues while offline; the simulation advances when the player returns, using time-based chunk replay.

Robots are modular constructs built from defined frames and components. They are designed as persistent assets, not disposable loadouts. To change a robot, the player schedules a refit or redesign at a factory, consuming time and capacity. This keeps mission deployment costly and makes long-term fleet architecture meaningful.

Missions execute without direct control. Robots follow declared instructions and return with outcomes and telemetry. Conflict, when it occurs, is log-resolved and treated as a systems validation event—not a reflex challenge. Success depends on preparation, resource allocation, and design quality.

## Planning, Prioritization, and the Process Layer

A defining feature of Dimesnship is that “planning” is not cosmetic: it is power.

A simple player order—such as “produce X components”—auto-expands into a production plan: input acquisition, logistics routing, facility usage, intermediate steps, and output storage. By default, the system auto-plans and runs.

But the player can drill down.

A process view presents active work as a priority-ordered list and expandable Gantt-style timelines. Each process attempts to execute in priority order each simulation chunk. If a required facility, resource, energy budget, or compute slot is unavailable, the process does not run and logs the reason. Inefficiency becomes visible as blocked time and empty cycles.

Advanced players can outperform autoplan by:

- Reordering priorities (drag-and-drop scheduling)
- Adding dependencies (“do this after that,” “allow parallel except first shipment”)
- Routing logistics through different transport lines
- Smoothing energy peaks
- Reducing wasted cycles and contention

The result is a core fantasy: a player who understands their system can achieve in hours what unstructured execution would take days to complete.

## Optimization Instead of “Research”

Dimesnship avoids implausible “remote invention.” Progress is framed as optimization: tuning manufacturing, logistics, and control systems to reduce waste, improve throughput, and unlock better scheduling tools.

Upgrades and optimization programs improve:

- Production efficiency and energy intensity
- Transport throughput and power cost
- Facility reconfiguration time (locking/setup duration)
- Compute capacity for scheduling and monitoring
- Mission readiness and report clarity

This keeps advancement grounded and aligned with the game’s industrial tone.

## Companions and Minor Automation Boons

The player is isolated. Over time they may acquire collectible “companion programs”—banned AI constructs existing as holographic crew substitutes. They are not required for progression and do not replace planning. They offer minor boons and personality:

- Additional compute capacity per simulation chunk
- Emergency recall triggers to limit damage
- Small productivity boosts in specific mission contexts
- Simple automation helpers (for example, repeating a mission using an assigned squad)
- Enhanced reporting and visual summaries

They are meant to support the control-console fantasy without turning the game into an idle auto-play machine.

## Tone and Identity

Dimesnship maintains a restrained, analytical tone. The player is not a heroic commander. They are a quiet strategist—building capability through patience, iteration, and precision.

Satisfaction comes from:

- Watching well-structured systems execute cleanly
- Tracing cause-and-effect through telemetry
- Correcting flawed plans and doctrines
- Discovering contradictions across time-offset strata that refine the truth

Dimesnship sits at the intersection of:

- Automation and optimization games
- Offline-friendly asynchronous progression
- Hard science-fiction systems simulation
- Investigative narrative through reconstruction

It is not spectacle-driven. It is controlled complexity.

## Vision Statement

Dimesnship delivers a deterministic, offline-first, asynchronous systems experience where the player architects autonomous operations across dimensional strata to survive, optimize, and optionally reconstruct truth from imperfect observation.

By making planning, prioritization, and analysis the primary mechanics, the game transforms industrial management and automation into narrative progression.

At its core, Dimesnship asks:

> Can you build systems robust enough to operate without you—  
> and precise enough to reveal what happened, even when you can’t prove it directly?
